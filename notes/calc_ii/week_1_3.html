Necessary condition for convergence

Series `S` converges   `=>`   `lim_(k->oo) b_k = 0`   N.B. the converse does not hold. i.e.   the harmonic series satisfies `lim_(k->oo) b_k = 0`, yet it diverges  

Absolute convergence

  `S = sum_(k=0)^(oo) b_k` converges absolutely   `<=>`   `sum_(k=0)^(oo) |b_k|` converges    [ `=> S` converges ]  

Ratio test for convergence

 
`r(b) = lim_(k->oo) |b_(k+1)/b_k|`   `r(b) < 1`   `=>`   `S` converges absolutely   `r(b) > 1`   `=>`   `S` diverges
  N.B. if `r(b)` does not exist, or `r(b) = 1`, the ratio test is inconclusive  

Ratio test applied to power series

Given the following definition of a power series `S(x) = sum_(k=0)^(oo) \ a_k \ (x-x_0)^k` we have that `r(b) = |(a_(k+1) \ (x-x_0)^(k+1))/(a_k \ (x-x_0)^k)|`   `= |a_(k+1)/a_k| \ * \ |x-x_0|`   `= r(a) * |x - x_0|` Thus, the above conditions may be rewritten for power series as `|x-x_0| < 1/(r(a))`   `=>`   `S` converges absolutely   `|x-x_0| > 1/(r(a))`   `=>`   `S` diverges (assuming `r(a)` exists) Therefore `R = 1/(r(a))` gives us the radius of convergence (RoC) of a power series. The inequality `|x-x_0| < R` gives us the region of convergence, which may be written as the interval `(x_0 - R, \ \ x_0 + R)`
