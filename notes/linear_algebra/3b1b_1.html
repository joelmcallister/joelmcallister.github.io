Matrices as linear transformations

A really nice intuition for a linear transformation is to think of it as replacement of our basis vectors (`i` and `j` when working in two dimensions) with a set of new basis vectors. Handily, we can then calculate the vector that `[(x), (y)] = x i + y j` is transformed to by replacing `i` and `j` with our new basis vectors: `3i + 7j to 3[(4), (2)] + 7[(3), (11)]`. This gives us an intuition for matrix-vector multiplication: `[(4, 3), (2, 11)][(3), (7)] = 3[(4), (2)] + 7[(3), (11)] = [(3 * 4 + 7 * 3), (3 * 2 + 7 * 11)] = [(33), (83)]`.  

Matrix multiplication as composition

This also gives an intuition for matrix multiplication: Consider `[(color(blue) 4, color(orange) 7), (color(blue) 3, color(orange) 4)] [(color(red) 3, color(green) 5), (color(red) 4, color(green) 8)]` Our first column in given by `[(color(blue) 4, color(orange) 7), (color(blue) 3, color(orange) 4)][color(red)((3), (4))]`, and our second column is given by `[(color(blue) 4, color(orange) 7), (color(blue) 3, color(orange) 4)][color(green)((5), (8))]`. In the above example, since `[color(red)((3), (4))]` and `[color(green)((5), (8))]` represent our transformed basis vectors, we can apply the second linear transformation to our transformed basis vectors to get our final basis vectors.
