Properties of determinants (`n xx n`)

  The following properties hold of determinants of `n xx n` matrices. `det (AB) = det(A) \ det(B)`   `det (A^T) = det(A)`     Multiplying any row or column of a matrix by a constant also multiplies the determinant by that same constant.   If a given row or column `i_0` in a matrix may be written as the sum of two other row/column vectors `bb a + bb b`, then the determinant of that matrix may be written as the sum of the determinants of two matrices, where `i_0` has been replaced with `bb a` and `bb b`, respectively.     If any row or column of a square matrix is `bb 0`, then the determinant is `0`.   If any two rows or any two columns of a square matrix are equal, then the determinant is `0`.     The determinant is unchanged if a multiple of one row is added to another, or a multiple of one column is added to another. The determinant switches sign if two rows or two columns switch place. This last property is really a special case of the following If two rows or two columns of a matrix are permuted using the permutation `sigma`, then the value of the determinant is multiplied by `"sg"(sigma)`.   N.B.   one can expand along any row/column of an `n xx n` matrix to compute its determinant,   as in the `3 xx 3` case.  

Inverse of a matrix (`n xx n`)

The inverse of an `n xx n` matrix is defined as follows `A^(-1) = ("adj"(A))/(det(A))`   N.B.   As in the `2 xx 2` case, `A^(-1)` is only defined when `det(A) != 0`. However, this is not usually the most efficient way to calculate the inverse of a matrix.
