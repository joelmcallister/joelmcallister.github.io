Eigenvectors and eigenvalues

  An eigenvector of a square matrix `A` is any non-zero vector `bb v` such that `A bb v = lambda bb v` . for some field value `lambda`, which is in turn referred to as the associated eigenvalue. The distinctive geometrical characteristic of an eigenvector is that its direction will not change under the corresponding linear transformation - its length will simply be scaled by some associated eigenvalue. Developing the above equation, we get `A bb v = (lambda I) \ bb v` `A bb v - (lambda I) \ bb v = bb 0` `(A - lambda I) \ bb v = bb 0` Since `bb v != bb 0` by definition, we may write `det(A - lambda I) = 0`.  

Diagonalization

  Concentrating for now on the `2 xx 2` case, consider the matrix `A = [(3, 1), (0, 2)]` which has linearly independent eigenvectors `bb b_1 = [(1), (0)]`, `bb b_2 = [(1), (-1)]` with eigenvalues `3` and `2`, respectively. Form a square matrix `P` with vectors `bb b_1` and `bb b_2` as the two columns. `P = [(1, 1), (0, -1)]` We may now use this matrix to change our basis vectors to `bb b_1` and `bb b_2`. (see 3B1B's excellent videos on change of basis and eigenvectors). `D = P^(-1)AP` Changing our basis vectors to eigenvectors has the advantage that only the diagonal of our matrix will be non-zero, since each basis vector is simply transformed into a scaled version of itself. As such, we refer to `D` as our diagonalized matrix. By noting that `D^n = P^(-1)A^nP`   `=> A^n = PD^nP^(-1)` (this can be proved by noting how adjacent `PP^(-1)` pairs cancel in the expansion of `D^n`) we now have a simple formula for the `n`th power of a diagonalizable matrix. Notice that `P^(-1)AP = [(3, 0), (0, 2)]`. Indeed, in general for any diagonalizable `2 xx 2` matrix `A`, and its eigenvector pair `P`, `P^(-1)AP = [(lambda, 0), (0, mu)]` where `lambda` and `mu` are the eigenvalues corresponding to each respective eigenvector in `P`. N.B. eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues must be linearly independent.
